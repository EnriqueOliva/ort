Albumentations Augmentation Techniques for
Person Segmentation
Albumentations Transforms Overview
Albumentations provides a wide range of image augmentations, broadly categorized into spatial
(geometric) transforms (e.g. flips, rotations, crops) and pixel transforms (color, blur, noise) 1 2 . Spatial
transforms must be applied identically to both image and mask (to preserve alignment), while pixel-only
transforms affect only the image. Key spatial transforms include flips ( HorizontalFlip ,
VerticalFlip ), rotations and scaling ( Affine / ShiftScaleRotate ), and crops ( RandomCrop ,
RandomResizedCrop ). Pixel transforms include color adjustments ( RandomBrightnessContrast ,
HueSaturationValue , ColorJitter ), blurs ( GaussianBlur , MotionBlur ), noise ( GaussNoise ),
and others. Weather or special effects (e.g. RandomFog , RandomRain ) also exist but are less common for
indoor portraits. Each transform has parameters (e.g. flip probability p , rotation angle limits) which control
its behavior. Albumentations recommends building pipelines incrementally: start with basic size
normalization (e.g. Resize ) and horizontal flips, then add more complex transforms one at a time 3 4 .

Geometric (Spatial) Transforms
• HorizontalFlip – Purpose: Mirror image left-right. Parameters: p (probability of applying). Use Case:
Introduce left-right invariance. For humans, horizontal flips create realistic mirrored poses. Caution:
Generally safe for segmentation (affects mask correspondingly) 5 . Example:
A.HorizontalFlip(p=0.5) .
• VerticalFlip – Purpose: Flip image top-bottom. Parameters: p . Use Case: Rarely needed; simulates
upside-down viewpoints. Caution: Avoid for upright humans (our dataset has no upside-down
people) – produces unnatural results. Example: A.VerticalFlip(p=0.5) .
• Affine / ShiftScaleRotate – Purpose: Apply rotation, scaling, translation, and optional shear in one
transform. Parameters: e.g. scale=(min,max) , rotate=(angle_min,angle_max) ,
translate_percent={"x":(...),"y":(...)} , fit_output . Use Case: Introduce mild
rotations (e.g. ±10–15°) and zoom (scale) variations. Caution: Keep ranges conservative for humans.
Large rotations (e.g. 45°) or flips can make poses unrealistic 6 7 . Example:
A.Affine(scale=(0.9,1.1), rotate=(-10,10), translate_percent={"x":(-0.1,
0.1),"y":(-0.1,0.1)}, fit_output=True, p=0.5)
• Perspective – Purpose: Apply a random 3D-like perspective warp. Parameters: scale=(min, max) ,
which controls the intensity. Use Case: Simulate slight camera perspective changes. Caution: Distorts
straight lines; avoid strong warping on people (bodies may bend unnaturally). Use small scale (e.g.
≤0.1). Example: A.Perspective(scale=(0.05, 0.1), p=0.3) .
• RandomCrop / RandomResizedCrop – Purpose: Crop a sub-region of the image
(RandomResizedCrop also resizes the crop). Parameters: output height,width , optionally scale

A.RandomCrop(height=384, width=384, p=1.0) .
• CenterCrop / SmallestMaxSize – Purpose: Deterministically crop/resize around center or resize
shortest side. Use Case: Normalize image size. Example: A.Resize(384, 384, p=1.0) or
A.SmallestMaxSize(max_size=800, p=1.0) .

Pixel (Color/Noise) Transforms
• RandomBrightnessContrast – Purpose: Randomly change brightness and contrast. Parameters:
brightness_limit , contrast_limit , typically fractions like ±0.2–0.3. Use Case: Simulate
different lighting exposures. Caution: Too large limits can over/under-expose images. Example:
A.RandomBrightnessContrast(brightness_limit=0.2, contrast_limit=0.2, p=0.3) .
• HueSaturationValue – Purpose: Adjust HSV channels (hue, saturation, value). Parameters:
hue_shift_limit , sat_shift_limit , val_shift_limit (e.g. ±10–20). Use Case: Vary color
tones and saturation, useful when dataset has rich color or also includes B/W. Caution: Has no effect
on pure grayscale images (hue shifts do nothing). Example:
A.HueSaturationValue(hue_shift_limit=10, sat_shift_limit=20,
val_shift_limit=20, p=0.3) .
• ColorJitter – Purpose: Simultaneously adjust brightness, contrast, saturation, hue (PyTorch-like).
Parameters: similar to above (e.g. brightness=0.3, contrast=0.3, saturation=0.3, hue=0.1) 8 . Use
Case: Comprehensive color augmentation. Caution: Duplicate of
RandomBrightnessContrast+HueSaturationValue; choose one strategy. Example:
A.ColorJitter(brightness=0.3, contrast=0.3, saturation=0.3, hue=0.1, p=0.5)
• CLAHE (Contrast Limited AHE) – Purpose: Locally equalize contrast (improves local details).
Parameters: clip_limit (e.g. 2.0), tile_grid_size (e.g. (8,8)). Use Case: Handle extreme
lighting (very dark/brighter regions). Caution: Can amplify noise or artifacts if overused. Example:
A.CLAHE(clip_limit=2.0, tile_grid_size=(8,8), p=0.2) .
• RandomGamma – Purpose: Apply random gamma correction. Parameters: gamma_limit (e.g.
(80,120) maps to γ∈[0.8,1.2]). Use Case: Change overall image brightness nonlinearly. Example:
A.RandomGamma(gamma_limit=(80,120), p=0.2) .
• ToGray – Purpose: Convert image to grayscale. Parameters: p . Use Case: Make model invariant to
color; simulate the ~3% black-white images in dataset. Caution: Removes all color cues, forcing
reliance on shape/texture

9

. Example: A.ToGray(p=0.1) .

• GaussNoise – Purpose: Add Gaussian noise to pixels. Parameters: var_limit (e.g. (0,10) for low
noise). Use Case: Simulate sensor noise. Caution: Our dataset is mostly clean (~3% noise); heavy noise
can degrade details. Use low probability or small variance. Example:
A.GaussNoise(var_limit=(0.0,10.0), p=0.1) .
• ISONoise – Purpose: Add realistic camera (ISO) noise with color shifts. Parameters: e.g.
intensity=(0.1,0.5) . Use Case: Advanced noise; rarely needed for high-quality photos. Caution:
Same as Gaussian noise. Example: A.ISONoise(color_shift=(0.01,0.05),
intensity=(0.1,0.5), p=0.1) .

• GaussianBlur / MotionBlur / MedianBlur – Purpose: Blur the image (average blur, motion blur,
median filter). Parameters: blur_limit or kernel sizes. Use Case: Simulate defocus or camera
motion blur. Caution: Edge-blurring: these soften object boundaries, which can harm segmentation
accuracy if overused. Use sparingly with small blur (e.g. kernel 3–5) or low probability. Example:
A.GaussianBlur(blur_limit=(3,5), p=0.1) .
• UnsharpMask / Sharpen – Purpose: Sharpen edges (unsharp mask) or local contrast (sharpen). Use
Case: Enhance edge definition. Caution: Use mild settings to avoid halos. Example:
A.UnsharpMask(blur_limit=3, p=0.2) .
• ImageCompression (JPEG) – Purpose: Simulate JPEG compression artifacts. Parameters:
quality_lower , quality_upper . Use Case: Robustness to varied image quality. Caution: Can
subtly blur fine detail. Example: A.ImageCompression(quality_lower=75,
quality_upper=100, p=0.1) .
• CoarseDropout (Cutout) – Purpose: Randomly mask out rectangular patches (cutout). Parameters:
number of holes, hole size. Use Case: Simulate occlusion (e.g. randomly covering part of a person),
and regularization. Caution: If holes cover large body parts, training can be harder. Use moderate
sizes. Example: A.CoarseDropout(max_holes=5, max_height=64, max_width=64, p=0.3) .
Citations above draw on Albumentations documentation and best-practice guides 1 5 3 6 . For
example, spatial flips and rotations are known to affect masks in sync 5 , and aggressive elastic or
distortion transforms are generally reserved for specialized domains (e.g. medical imaging) 10 and may be
harmful for natural human figures. Color transforms like ToGray force shape learning 9 , which is useful
when color cues are inconsistent.

Segmentation-Specific Best Practices
• Sync Image & Mask: Always use A.Compose([...], additional_targets={'mask':
'mask'}) so that spatial transforms (flips, crops, rotations) apply to the mask with nearestneighbor interpolation. Albumentations defaults to nearest neighbor for masks to avoid
introducing spurious classes 11 12 . (If using bicubic on a binary mask, you could create invalid
label values.)
• Avoid Unnecessary Complexity: Start with minimal augmentation (resize/crop + basic flip) and
gradually add more. Adding many strong transforms at once can create unrealistic samples 3 .
Monitor validation Dice – if adding a transform hurts performance, scale it back.
• Maintain Edges: Pixel-level transforms that blur or distort edges (Gaussian blur, JPEG artifacts,
elastic warps) should be used judiciously. Such transforms can reduce sharpness of object
boundaries; for segmentation tasks, preserving boundary detail is important. If color noise or blur is
needed, keep the effect mild.

Person-Specific Augmentation Strategies
• Orientation Constraints: Since all people in the dataset are upright, do not use vertical flips or
180° rotations. Horizontal flips are safe (people are roughly symmetric left-right). Use small-angle
rotations (e.g. ±15° or less) if needed, as larger rotations rarely occur in upright portraits 6 .
• Proportions and Poses: Avoid transforms that warp human proportions unnaturally. For example,
elastic or grid distortions can stretch limbs and body shapes, which are unrealistic; these should be
omitted or set with very low probability 10 . Simple affine (scale/rotate) with limited range is better.

• Scale Variation: If people can appear at different distances, you can slightly scale or zoom images
(e.g. A.RandomResizedCrop or A.Affine(scale=(0.9,1.1)) ). However, do not zoom so
much that the person is cut off by the image border.
• Occlusions: Simulating mild occlusions (with CoarseDropout ) can improve robustness (e.g.
someone holding an object), but avoid covering the entire head/body.

Color and Grayscale Augmentation
• Mixed Color/BW Dataset: Since ~3% of images are black-and-white, the model should not rely solely
on color. Use A.ToGray(p=0.1–0.2) to randomly convert some color images to grayscale 9 .
Also, channel dropout/shuffle can encourage focusing on shape over color (e.g.
A.ChannelDropout(p=0.1) ).
• Color Variations: Apply modest color augmentations to account for different camera filters and
lighting. Useful transforms include RandomBrightnessContrast and HueSaturationValue .
For example, RandomBrightnessContrast(brightness_limit=0.2, contrast_limit=0.2)
and HueSaturationValue(hue_shift_limit=10, sat_shift_limit=20,
val_shift_limit=20) vary color modestly

ColorJitter (Albumentations) or ChannelShuffle can be used.
• Color Jitter / Channel Drop: Some pipelines use ColorJitter or drop color channels with
A.ChannelDropout to force the model to use texture/shape. These help prevent overfitting to
specific color patterns.

Lighting and Contrast Augmentation
• Brightness/Contrast: A.RandomBrightnessContrast is a good default for simulating lighting
changes. Set limits conservatively (e.g. ±0.2) to avoid clipping.
• Gamma Adjustment: Use A.RandomGamma to simulate exposure differences (e.g.
gamma_limit=(80,120) ). This can make dark areas darker or lighter globally.
• CLAHE: For high-dynamic-range scenes or silhouettes, A.CLAHE can enhance local contrast. Set
clip_limit around 2.0. This can help recover details in shadows or highlights.
• Shadow/Glare Effects: Albumentations has RandomShadow , RandomSunFlare , etc., but these
are more suited to outdoors or autonomous-driving data. For studio/portrait photos, they may
introduce unrealistic artifacts; use only if relevant.

Edge Preservation and Blur Avoidance
• Avoid Heavy Blur: GaussianBlur , MotionBlur , or strong compression blur edges and may
hinder segmentation. Use very mild blur (kernel 3–5) with low probability if at all. As an alternative,
you can use sharpening ( UnsharpMask or Sharpen ) to slightly enhance edges, but overdoing it
creates halos.
• Elastic/Grid Distortion: These distort object shapes smoothly but non-uniformly. While
Albumentations lists them as Mixed or advanced transforms 13 , they are not recommended for
human body masks because they will create anatomically impossible limb warps. If you need small
non-linear warps, consider limiting ElasticTransform(alpha=..., sigma=...) with very
small alpha.

• Edge Preservation Tip: Always visualize augmented images. Ensure that after augmentation, the

break the “naturalness” of a body shape.

Albumentations Pipeline Composition
• Ordering: Place spatial transforms (crop, flip, rotate) early, and pixel transforms (color, noise) later.
For performance, put any expensive or blurring transforms at the end 14 15 . Always include a final
A.Normalize (and ToTensorV2 ) after all random augmentations.
• OneOf / SomeOf: Use A.OneOf([...], p) to randomly choose one transform from a list. For
example, A.OneOf([A.GaussianBlur(p=1.0), A.MotionBlur(p=1.0)], p=0.5) applies
either a Gaussian or motion blur (90% chance from [41†L1082-L1090]). Similarly,
A.SomeOf([...], n=(1,2), p) can apply a random subset of transforms 16

limit how many augmentations stack at once and add randomness. For instance, group color
changes:
A.OneOf([
A.RandomBrightnessContrast(p=1.0),

], p=0.5)
• Sequential / RandomOrder: Albumentations also offers Sequential (apply a block of transforms
together with one probability) and RandomOrder (shuffle the order of listed transforms each time)

ordering with varied probabilities is usually sufficient.
• Probability Tuning: Common practice is to set moderate probabilities (p=0.3–0.5) for each random
augmentation. However, some invariances should always apply (e.g. Resize(p=1.0) , or flips at
p=0.5). Use higher p if a transform is safe and useful, lower if it’s riskier.

Domain-Specific Recommendations
• Portrait/Fashion Context: Augmentations should respect the photographic style. For studio
portraits, heavy weather effects (rain, snow) are unlikely and can be omitted. For outdoor shots,
moderate lighting and blur adjustments may simulate sun/shade.
• Background Diversity: The dataset has varied backgrounds (urban, nature, studio). You could
simulate background variation with color jitter and brightness changes. Some Kagglers also suggest
random background blending (inserting a subject into a different scene), but that is complex and
dataset-specific.
• Professional vs Amateur: Professional photos tend to have higher contrast and dynamic range. To
mimic amateur shots, you might add slight noise or underexposure. Conversely, to be robust to pro
settings, avoid too-easy normalization (use mild augmentations only).
• Portrait Poses: If the dataset includes full-body fashion poses, consider small perspective changes
(a very slight tilt) but not strong affine flips. Ensure neck-body alignment remains realistic.

Performance Considerations
• Crop Early: Always crop or resize before heavy augmentations. Cropping an image to the target size
first can reduce the number of pixels processed by subsequent steps by up to ~16× 15 . For example,
apply RandomResizedCrop or RandomCrop at the start.
• Data Types: Use uint8 images for Albumentations transforms when possible 15 . Perform all
augmentations on uint8 and only normalize (to float) as the final step. This leverages OpenCV’s
optimization.
• Threads and IO: In PyTorch DataLoaders, set cv2.setNumThreads(0) in worker init to avoid
OpenCV thread conflicts 15 . Use efficient image loading (cv2 or torchvision.io ) instead of
Pillow for speed.
• Combine Simple Transforms: Use combined transforms like A.Affine rather than separate
Rotate , Scale , Translate calls, to reduce overhead 15 .
• GPU Augmentation: Albumentations runs on CPU. If GPU-accelerated augmentations are needed,
consider libraries like Kornia or NVIDIA DALI. However, for our moderate batch size (8 on RTX 4070),
Albumentations on CPU (with multiple workers) is typically adequate.
• Benchmarking: Always monitor data loading time. If augmentation becomes a bottleneck, profile
which transforms are slow (elastic, perspective, large images) and move them later or cache results.

This pipeline resizes images, applies horizontal flips and mild affine warps, then color/contrast changes
(brighter/darker or color shifts), occasional grayscale conversion, minor gamma/C LA HE adjustments for
lighting, very light blur or noise, and some coarse dropout for occlusion. Vertical flips and extreme elastic/
perspective are omitted. All spatial transforms will apply identically to the mask (maintaining alignment) 5
12 .

Transforms to Remove / Add
• Remove from current pipeline: Based on dataset and issues:
• A.VerticalFlip : drop it (people never upside-down).
• A.ShiftScaleRotate with wide rotate limits (30°): either narrow the angle (e.g. 15°) or replace
with A.Affine with smaller range.
• A.ElasticTransform : remove or set alpha extremely low; it distorts human shapes 10 .
• Heavy noise: since most images are clean, high-variance

GaussNoise

• Add based on dataset:
• Color jittering: A.HueSaturationValue or A.ColorJitter (as shown) to handle varied color
tones.
• Grayscale conversion: A.ToGray to force robustness to B/W.
• Gamma/CLAHE: to handle extreme lighting/silhouette cases.
• Mild affine/perspective: if needed, ensure safe parameters ( A.Affine(rotate=(-15,15)) ).
• RandomCrop/ResizedCrop: if some images have lot of background, random cropping can help
focus on person (with caution not to remove head).
• CoarseDropout: simulate occlusion (small probability).
• ChannelDropout: optionally to ignore color (complementing ToGray ).
• OneOf wrappers: group similar ops (e.g. one blur or one noise at a time) to add variety without
overwhelming each image.
